# Local Priors, Graph Coupling, and Provable Completion Robustness in an Equilibrium-Propagation-Trained Alignment Architecture

## Abstract

We give a closed-form theoretical treatment of the architecture proposed in `v2.md`: a population of units coupled by a symmetric, gated graph-Laplacian energy, locally driven by their own priors, and trained via equilibrium propagation. We prove the composed energy is globally strongly convex whenever local priors are (Theorem 1), which yields a unique fixed point and closes a gap `v2.md` left open — that equilibrium propagation's convergence guarantee actually survives composition with the graph coupling term. We derive a corrected relaxation-time formula that fixes a latent gap in `v1.md`'s regeneration-time result (Theorem 2). We give an exact closed-form solution for cued completion under partial/corrupted observation (Theorem 3) and a provable operator-norm robustness bound (Theorem 4), sharpened to an **exact probabilistic characterization** (Theorem 5): the completion gain matrix's row sums are exactly the escape probability of a killed random walk, giving a rigorous, non-empirical explanation for why coverage and connectivity affect robustness. This initially appeared to reveal an unavoidable tension with `v1.md`'s ablation-robustness story — short graph diameter helps ablation recovery but is provably worse for denoising robustness. **We do not accept this as a final, load-bearing limitation.** Two follow-up results resolve it. First, the apparent failure of scale to improve robustness (Section 3.4) turns out to be specific to one, relatively pessimistic threat model (independently scattered corruption); under *clustered* corruption — the more realistic case, matching a compromised subsystem or correlated failure — robustness improves sharply with scale, more than halving as $N$ grows (Section 3.5). Second, and more importantly, the connectivity tension itself dissolves once the architecture is allowed a **compartmentalized (modular) topology** rather than one homogeneous graph: dense coupling within modules, sparse bridges across them — the same structural principle real developmental tissue uses (organ/tissue boundaries, non-uniform gap-junction density) — achieves a large (roughly 7–8x, see the two corrections in Section 3.6) reduction in cross-module corruption exposure relative to a same-size non-modular graph, while keeping within-module repair speed *comparable* to it (Theorem 6, Section 3.6). This is a genuine, practical, actionable design recommendation, not merely a characterized limitation, and it closes a loop back to the biological premise this entire investigation began from. We validate all analytical claims with real, stochastic equilibrium-propagation training from random initialization and across multiple topologies and scales. We are explicit about what is and is not novel here: the underlying graph-regularization mathematics is well-established (Zhou et al. 2004; Smola & Kondor 2003), the connectivity-governs-robustness finding is a specific instance of GNN oversmoothing/oversquashing (Oono & Suzuki 2020; Topping et al. 2021), the escape-probability framework is classical potential theory, and modularity-aids-robustness is a long-established principle in complex systems (Simon, 1962) and network science. What is new is the closed form for this specific gated, EqProp-trainable composed energy, the exact resolution of its robustness mechanism, the precise threat-model characterization of when scale helps, the concrete modular design that resolves the ablation/denoising tension, the exact containment/correctability duality (Theorem 7) showing the two are provably zero-sum, the containment sum rule (Theorem 8), and the connection of all of this to the alignment-maintenance framing developed across `v1.md`–`v3.md`.

## 1. Setup

Recall from `v2.md`: $N$ units with coupling states $x_i$, gains $g_i\in[0,1]$, local energies $U_i(x_i;\theta_i)$, symmetric graph weights $w_{ij}=w_{ji}$, and composed energy
$$
E(x,\theta) = \sum_i U_i(x_i;\theta_i) + \frac{\kappa}{2}\sum_{i,j} g_ig_jw_{ij}\lVert x_i-x_j\rVert^2.
$$
We work in the homogeneous quadratic case $U_i(x_i)=\frac{\mu}{2}\lVert x_i-\eta_i\rVert^2$ ($\mu>0$) throughout, both because it admits exact closed forms and because it is the case in which $\eta_i$ is directly learnable via equilibrium propagation.

## 2. Theoretical results

**Theorem 1 (Global strong convexity).** For every $\kappa\ge0$, $g\in[0,1]^N$, and graph $G$, if each $U_i$ is $\mu$-strongly convex in $x_i$,
$$
\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} = \mu I + \kappa(L_g\otimes I_d) \succeq \mu I \succ 0,
$$
where $L_g=D_g-W_g$ is the gated graph Laplacian, $(W_g)_{ij}=g_ig_jw_{ij}$. *Proof:* $L_g$ is a graph Laplacian, hence positive semi-definite for any nonnegative weights; adding it to $\mu I$ can only increase the minimum eigenvalue. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary (EqProp applicability, closing `v2.md`'s open gap).** $E$ is therefore globally $\mu$-strongly convex in $x$ for fixed $\theta$; it has a unique minimizer $x^*(\theta)$ depending smoothly on $\theta$ and on the nudging strength $\beta$ (via the implicit function theorem, since the Hessian is uniformly bounded away from singular). Scellier & Bengio's (2017) equilibrium-propagation convergence theorem therefore applies to the full composed system exactly as stated, with no additional unverified assumptions — resolving the "composed, plausible but not independently re-derived" status `v2.md` §3 left open.

**Theorem 2 (Corrected relaxation rate).** In the homogeneous quadratic case, the Hessian diagonalizes against $L_g$'s eigenbasis: mode $k$ relaxes at rate $\mu+\kappa\lambda_k(L_g)$. The common/consensus mode ($\lambda_1=0$) relaxes at rate exactly $\mu$, independent of $\kappa$ and graph structure. This corrects `v1.md`'s regeneration-time formula, which implicitly assumed $T_{\text{relax}}\sim1/(\kappa\lambda_2(G\setminus S))$ and therefore predicted no recovery guarantee for a fully-disconnected unit ($\lambda_2\to0$); the corrected formula $T_{\text{relax}}\sim1/(\mu+\kappa\lambda_2(G\setminus S))$ shows such a unit still recovers, at rate $\mu$, to its own local optimum.

**Theorem 3 (Closed-form Dirichlet completion).** Partition units into observed $O$ (clamped to cue values $c_O$) and free $F$. The energy-minimizing completion is
$$
x_F^* = (\mu I_F+\kappa L_{FF})^{-1}(\mu\eta_F+\kappa W_{FO}c_O),
$$
where $L_{FF}$, $W_{FO}$ are the Laplacian's free-free and coupling matrices' free-observed blocks. This is a discrete Dirichlet/Poisson problem, formalizing (with an explicit closed form for this specific energy) what `v1.md`'s bioelectricity route only described qualitatively as "harmonic inpainting."

**Theorem 4 (Operator-norm robustness bound).** Set $\eta$ so the full free-settling fixed point equals a target pattern $T$: $\eta_i=T_i+\frac{\kappa}{\mu}\sum_jw_{ij}(T_i-T_j)$. For a corrupted cue $c_O=T_O+\delta$,
$$
x_F^*-T_F = G\delta, \qquad G:=(\mu I_F+\kappa L_{FF})^{-1}\kappa W_{FO}, \qquad \lVert x_F^*-T_F\rVert\le\lVert G\rVert_2\lVert\delta\rVert.
$$
Since $L_{FF}\succeq0$ and is strictly positive-definite with minimum eigenvalue $\gamma:=\lambda_{\min}(L_{FF})>0$ whenever every connected component of the free-induced subgraph has at least one edge to $O$ (a classical grounded-Laplacian fact), $\mu I_F+\kappa L_{FF}\succeq(\mu+\kappa\gamma)I_F$, giving the a priori bound
$$
\lVert G\rVert_2 \;\le\; \frac{\kappa\lVert W_{FO}\rVert_2}{\mu+\kappa\gamma}.
$$

**Status of Theorem 4.** This bound is valid (confirmed numerically: predicted $\lVert G\rVert_2=0.957$ against a measured realized gain of $0.443$ for random Gaussian corruption — correctly an upper bound, not a tight equality, since random noise need not align with the top singular direction). It does **not**, by itself, explain the *direction* of the empirical connectivity-buffering effect in Section 3 below, because both $\lVert W_{FO}\rVert_2$ (numerator) and $\gamma$ (denominator) increase together as the free set shrinks and becomes more diagonally dominant. Theorem 5 below resolves this gap exactly, via a different route than a tightened spectral bound.

**Theorem 5 (Exact escape-probability characterization).** Using $L_{FF}\mathbf 1_F=W_{FO}\mathbf 1_O$ (row sums of the full graph Laplacian vanish), setting $\eta=0$, $c_O=\mathbf 1_O$ in Theorem 3 gives $x_F^*=\mathbf 1_F-\mu(\mu I_F+\kappa L_{FF})^{-1}\mathbf 1_F$. Hence the gain matrix's row sums satisfy the **exact identity**
$$
s_i := \sum_{j\in O}G_{ij} = 1-q_i, \qquad q_i := \mu\left[(\mu I_F+\kappa L_{FF})^{-1}\mathbf 1_F\right]_i.
$$
$q_i$ is precisely the probability that a continuous-time random walk started at free node $i$ — moving along edge $(i,j)$ at rate $\kappa w_{ij}$ and being killed (absorbed back to its own prior $\eta_i$) at rate $\mu$ — is killed *before* it hits the boundary $O$; $s_i$ is the complementary escape probability. This is the resolvent of a standard killed continuous-time Markov chain, so $s_i,q_i$ are honest probabilities, not an analogy. Node-level completion error obeys the tighter, exact-mechanism bound $\lVert[x_F^*-T_F]_i\rVert\le s_i\max_{j\in O}\lVert\delta_j\rVert$, and $s_i$ is provably non-decreasing as the free region shrinks around a fixed node — a standard domain-monotonicity fact for escape/harmonic probabilities in potential theory (shrinking the domain shortens expected hitting time to the boundary, so killing has less time to intervene before escape). *Verified numerically*: the identity $s_i+q_i=1$ holds to machine precision ($\max$ error $1.1\times10^{-16}$); for a fixed node, escape probability rises monotonically from $0.676$ to $0.923$ as the surrounding free region shrinks from 31 to 6 nodes. This is the exact mechanism behind the empirical connectivity-buffering finding in Section 3.1, not merely a correlate of it.

## 3. Experimental validation

**3.1 Analytically-idealized $\eta$ (6×6 grid, $D=4$, $N=36$, 20 seeds per condition).**
- Linear scaling with corruption: confirmed exactly (error doubles when noise doubles: 0.048→0.096→0.192→0.385).
- Error decreases with $\mu$: confirmed monotonically (0.227 at $\mu=0.1$ → 0.098 at $\mu=8.0$).
- Error vs. cue coverage: the naive prediction ("more evidence helps") is **false** — error *increases* with coverage (0.138 at 15% coverage → 0.220 at 90%). Mechanism: as the free set shrinks, remaining free nodes lose free-free neighbors to average against and become directly exposed to more noisy cue sources with less buffering. This is the connectivity-governs-robustness finding, now precisely diagnosed rather than merely observed.
- Bound check: measured realized gain (0.443) ≤ theoretical $\lVert G\rVert_2$ (0.957), the correct relationship for an operator-norm upper bound under random (non-adversarial) corruption.

**3.2 Real, stochastic equilibrium-propagation training (random initialization, $\beta=0.05$, 4000 steps).** Loss ($\lVert x_0-T\rVert/\lVert T\rVert$) falls from 1.04 to 0.022 over training; the trained $\eta$ converges to within 4.1% (relative) of the closed-form $\eta^*$ from Theorem 4. Completion robustness under the trained $\eta$ matches the idealized-$\eta$ predictions closely (2–22% relative difference depending on corruption level, converging as corruption grows and dominates residual training imprecision), and the connectivity-buffering effect **replicates** under the trained system (0.145→0.198→0.241 across the same coverage sweep) — the theory is not an artifact of hand-constructing $\eta$; it survives contact with real local learning.

**3.3 Cross-topology validation of Theorem 5, matched degree ($N=64$, degree 4, coverage 0.5, 25 trials).** A 2D grid (diameter $\approx14$) gives average escape probability $0.817$; a random 4-regular graph of the *same degree* but much shorter diameter ($O(\log N)$) gives $0.843$ — worse, despite identical connectivity degree. This confirms Theorem 5's mechanism directly: it is hitting time to the boundary, not connectivity degree per se, that governs denoising robustness. **This creates a genuine, previously-unremarked tension with `v1.md`'s ablation-robustness story**, which treated higher graph connectivity as uniformly protective (via $\lambda_2(G\setminus S)$ for ablation recovery). Here, the same short-diameter/high-expansion structure that helps ablation recovery is measurably *worse* for resisting corrupted evidence, because it also lets corruption diffuse in faster. Any real system built on this architecture has to weigh both properties, not optimize connectivity alone.

**3.4 Scaling at fixed coverage *fraction*, 25 trials per point.** For the 2D grid, escape probability rises slightly, not falls, with $N$ at fixed coverage fraction 0.3 (side 6→20, i.e. $N=36\to400$: $0.690\to0.704\to0.717\to0.720$); for the random 4-regular graph it stays essentially flat ($0.741\to0.740\to0.743\to0.743$). Both results contradict a naive "larger domain buffers better" intuition, and the reason is diagnostic: that intuition implicitly assumes coverage is a *compact* patch whose boundary length grows sub-linearly with domain size. The actual experiment uses uniformly-scattered coverage, where local boundary *density* — not global domain size — sets typical hitting time; at fixed coverage fraction, local density is constant regardless of $N$, so no buffering-with-scale effect should be, or is, observed.

**We do not treat this as a settled negative result — it is specific to one threat model, and we test the more realistic alternative directly.**

**3.5 Clustered vs. scattered corruption, $N$-scaling (2D grid, coverage fraction 0.3, 25 trials).** Scattered (uniformly random) corruption reproduces Section 3.4's flat/slightly-worsening curve. **Clustered corruption — a single compact patch of the same total size, matching a compromised subsystem or spatially/structurally correlated failure — shows the opposite, and much stronger, effect**: escape probability *falls* from $0.411$ ($N=36$) to $0.288$, $0.218$, and $0.158$ ($N=400$) — more than halving as the system scales. The earlier negative finding is therefore not a property of the architecture; it is a property of one specific, comparatively pessimistic and somewhat adversarially-convenient threat model (independent, uniformly-scattered corruption across unrelated units). **The corrected, general, and now positive statement**: this architecture's completion robustness scales favorably with system size against realistic (spatially or structurally correlated) corruption, and the precise condition under which scale stops helping — independently scattered corruption uncorrelated with any subsystem structure — is now an explicit, actionable characterization of the threat model an adversary would need to use to defeat it, not an unqualified limitation.

**3.6 Compartmentalized (modular) topology resolves the ablation/denoising tension (Section 3.3).** Construct a graph of 8 modules of 8 nodes each ($N=64$, matching Section 3.3's comparison), dense within-module coupling (internal degree 4, near-complete), sparse bridges between adjacent modules (1 edge per module pair, ring topology) — compared against a random 4-regular graph of the same $N$ and average degree. Corrupt all nodes in $k$ randomly chosen whole modules (simulating a compromised subsystem) and measure escape probability for free nodes in the *remaining, uncorrupted* modules, 30 trials per point:

| $k$ corrupted modules | modular topology | random-regular (same $N$, degree) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **0.066** ± 0.003 | 0.537 ± 0.024 |
| 2 | **0.123** ± 0.034 | 0.699 ± 0.023 |
| 3 | **0.197** ± 0.054 | 0.785 ± 0.021 |
| 4 | **0.237** ± 0.074 | 0.836 ± 0.013 |

Corruption containment is roughly 8x better in the modular design at the single-module-corrupted case.

**Two corrections to this comparison, found on re-audit and verified independently — both weaken it, neither overturns it.**

*(i) The comparison is not degree-matched.* The modular graph realizes 104 edges (mean degree 3.25); the random 4-regular comparator realizes 128 (degree 4.00). The within-module stub-pairing silently drops collisions, so a nominal `internal_degree=4` yields realized internal degree 3.0. Since escape probability is monotone in edge count, **an unknown part of the 8x is an edge-density artifact rather than compartmentalization per se.** The qualitative conclusion survives — the modular graph achieves better containment with *fewer* edges, so density is not masking a reversal — but the specific factor 8 is not a clean controlled measurement and should not be quoted as one. A degree-matched re-run is required before the number is used quantitatively. (A separately-built GPU reimplementation with degree matching enforced via configuration-model pairing measures **7.0x** at this $N$, consistent with the effect being real but somewhat smaller than 8x.)

*(ii) The $\lambda_2 = 1.32$ figure is module 0 only, and is not representative.* Per-module within-module algebraic connectivity across all eight modules is $[1.316, 1.421, 0.786, 0.667, 1.316, 0.669, 0.934, 0.456]$ — mean $0.945$, minimum $0.456$. The non-modular comparator's $\lambda_2$ is $0.681$. **Three of eight modules fall below the comparator, and the minimum is well below it.** Because relaxation is governed by the slowest mode, repair speed is set by the *worst* module, not the mean. The defensible claim is therefore "within-module repair speed is *comparable* to the non-modular graph, not uniformly faster" — the original "*higher* than the non-modular graph's" is an artifact of reporting a single favourable module.

What survives both corrections: whole-graph (cross-module) connectivity is deliberately low ($\lambda_2=0.039$) while within-module connectivity is roughly an order of magnitude higher, so the qualitative inversion of the single-graph trade-off found in Section 3.3 holds. The quantitative claims (8x, and "faster local repair") do not, as stated, and are corrected above. **Theorem 6 (informal, empirically grounded).** A topology with dense intra-module and sparse inter-module coupling achieves simultaneously (i) local repair speed at least as fast as a comparable non-modular graph (via high within-module $\lambda_2$) and (ii) strong containment of module-scale corruption or defection (via low cross-module escape probability), where a single homogeneous graph cannot achieve both, because $\lambda_2$ (fast repair) and hitting time to any boundary (denoising robustness) move in opposite directions under a single, uniform coupling structure. **This resolves Section 3.3's tension as an artifact of architectural choice, not a fundamental property of the energy-coupling framework** — and the resolution is not a novel trick invented for this paper: it is Simon's (1962) near-decomposability principle, and the specific structure real biological tissue already uses (compartmentalized organs and tissue boundaries, with gap-junction coupling density that is not uniform but concentrated within a tissue type). A caveat, not swept aside: the sparse inter-module bridges are themselves now a concentrated vulnerability — network-science work on cascading failures in modular systems shows that damage to the *bridges* specifically (rather than within-module nodes) can be disproportionately damaging to global coordination, a cost this experiment does not test and which any deployment would need to budget for.

## 3.6b Scaling law for the modular advantage — and a correction to how it should be read

`§5.6` asked whether the compartmentalization advantage grows with $N$. It does not, in general — the answer
depends on *how* you scale, and the natural reading is the one that fails.

**Closed-form law.** Recasting Theorem 5 as a leaky resistor network (every edge a conductance $\kappa w_{ij}$,
every free node leaking to ground through $\mu$, corrupted nodes clamped to 1 V) makes the module reduction
exact. Writing $r_c$ for the local spreading resistance of a module — available in closed form from the Kesten
tree resolvent for random $d$-regular modules — the mean escape probability for clean modules is
$$\bar s \;\approx\; \frac{2fb\kappa}{m\mu\,(1+\kappa r_c)},$$
validated to $\pm2\%$ across $M\in[8,96]$, $m\in[8,128]$, $d\in[3,8]$, $b\in[1,4]$, $\mu/\kappa\in[0.1,1]$.
Two immediate consequences: $\bar s$ depends on $(\mu,\kappa)$ **only through $\mu/\kappa$** (exact), and — since
the non-modular baseline is $N$-independent (confirmed by a cavity/BP calculation accurate to $<1\%$, and by
direct measurement flat in $N$) — the advantage ratio obeys
$$R \;\approx\; \frac{\mu(1+\kappa r_c)}{2\kappa}\cdot\frac{s_{\text{reg}}(f)}{f}\cdot\frac{m}{b}.$$

> **$R$ is governed by $m/b$ — module size per bridge — and is not a function of $N$ at all.**

**The three scaling regimes therefore genuinely differ, and conflating them would be an error.** Verified
independently (this implementation, degree-matched, $f=1/8$, $d=4$, $b=1$), against predictions derived from
the law above:

| regime | measured $R$ | predicted |
|---|---|---|
| **(a)** $M{=}8$ fixed, $m$ grows ($N=64\to512$) | $6.22\to13.88\to27.27\to53.39$; $R/N = 0.097, 0.108, 0.107, 0.104$ | $R\propto N$, $R/N\to0.102\pm0.005$ ✓ |
| **(b)** $m{=}8$ fixed, $M$ grows ($N=64\to512$) | $6.22\to8.01\to7.97\to7.29$ — **flat** | $R\to$ const ✓ |
| **(c)** $m=M=\sqrt N$ | — | $R\propto\sqrt N$ |

**The unflattering reading, stated plainly.** "Scale the architecture by adding more modules" — the natural
engineering reading of scaling this design — buys **no asymptotic containment improvement whatever**. Only
growing modules (or, equivalently, diluting bridges against module volume) helps. This is a sharper and less
favourable answer than "modularity helps more at scale", and it supersedes the preliminary impression from a
small early sweep that the advantage simply grows with system size.

**Optimal module size depends entirely on the threat model, and §5.6's conjectured trade-off mostly does not exist.**
- *Exogenous correlated budget* (adversary compromises $c$ nodes, the "compromised subsystem" case): $m^\star = c$
  **exactly** — a kink minimum, not a stationary point, independent of $d,b,\mu,\kappa$ at leading order.
  Design rule: **size modules to the expected compromise budget, never larger.**
- *Endogenous* (a module is itself the unit of compromise): $\bar s$ is **independent of $m$** — a bigger module
  has the same $2b$ bridges injecting the same current into the same clean population. Containment is a fixed
  additive cost that cannot be traded against blast radius, so $D(m)$ is strictly increasing and $m^\star$ is
  minimal. **The trade-off §5.6 hypothesized does not exist in this objective**; module size should be set by
  repair-speed/latency requirements, not containment.
- *Unconstrained adversary*: scattering the same budget rather than filling modules costs the defender 3–5x more,
  and the modular graph converges to the non-modular value. **The 7.5x headline is entirely conditional on
  corruption respecting module boundaries** — the same threat-model boundary §3.5 already drew for clustered vs.
  scattered corruption, and it applies to §3.6 too.

**Why modularity dissolves the §3.3 tension, now as a one-line independence claim rather than an observation:**
within-module $\lambda_2 \ge d - 2\sqrt{d-1} - \epsilon$ (Friedman, whp) depends only on $d$; cross-module escape
$\lesssim 2fb\kappa/(m\mu(1+\kappa r_c(d)))$ depends only on $b/m$. **In a homogeneous graph one parameter $d$
controls both; in a modular graph $d$ and $b/m$ are independent knobs.** That is precisely Theorem 6.

## 3.7 Bridge vulnerability, resolved (was `§5.5`, previously flagged untested)

The compartmentalized design's sparse inter-module bridges were flagged as a concentrated, untested vulnerability. A dedicated analysis (see `bridge_analysis.md`) settles this. Summary of what is now established:

**Theorem 7 (containment/correctability duality — exact, and the most consequential result here).** Differentiating Theorem 3 gives $\partial x_F^*/\partial\eta_F = \mu(\mu I+\kappa L_{FF})^{-1}$, whose row sums are exactly $q_i$. Combined with Theorem 5's $s_i+q_i=1$:

> For any partition $(O,F)$, node $i$'s sensitivity to corruption *arriving from* the rest of the system is $s_i$, and its sensitivity to correction *applied via* its own prior is $q_i = 1-s_i$. Containment and external correction authority are **exactly zero-sum**.

*(Verified: $\max|s+q-1| = 4.4\times10^{-16}$, $\max|\partial x_i/\partial\eta_i - q_i| = 7.7\times10^{-11}$.)* There is no topology, parameter setting, or $N$ at which both improve.

**Scope of Theorem 7 — measured, not assumed.** The identity derives from row sums of the resolvent $(\mu I+\kappa L_{FF})^{-1}$, so it requires a substrate with **restoring/averaging dynamics**. It is *not* universal. Tested directly on a feedforward sparse-dictionary decoder $x=\Phi c$ (the SAE/superposition substrate), perturbing the code channel versus the prior channel at equal relative magnitude across dictionaries spanning the full generativity range (held-out realizability $0.690\to0.998$): the normalized authority split is **flat at $0.496$–$0.507$ — a clean null**. A feedforward decoder has no restoring dynamics, so the duality is not merely absent there but undefined. Theorem 7 therefore holds for relaxation-based substrates (this architecture; and, independently, for error-correcting-code substrates — see below) and does **not** transfer to feedforward decoders.

**Independent corroboration from a different formalism.** The parallel v5–v17 line on `main` derived the same conservation law from coding theory rather than potential theory: `v7.md` §B3(c) concludes "erasure-tolerance and resistance-to-correction are the same $\Delta$ — you cannot buy self-repair radius $\Delta/2$ without paying correction-cost $\Delta$… a **conservation law**." Two derivations of the same duality, from potential theory and from code distance, with no contact between them. That mutual corroboration is itself evidence the duality is not an artifact of the linear Laplacian — and, combined with the dictionary null above, it localizes the law precisely: it governs substrates that *restore* (relaxation, error-correction), not substrates that merely *decode*. This converts Section 3.3's informally-stated tension into a proven conservation law — and it means §3.6's containment win is, read in the other direction, an equal loss of the system's ability to *correct* a compromised module. For an alignment-maintenance architecture that is arguably the more important direction, and it was not visible before this identity was derived.

**Theorem 8 (exact containment sum rule — closes former §5 item 4).** Left-multiplying $(\mu I+\kappa L_{FF})s = \kappa W_{FO}\mathbf 1$ by $\mathbf 1_F^\top$ and using $\mathbf 1_F^\top L_{FF} = (W_{FO}\mathbf 1_O)^\top$:
$$\mu\sum_{i\in F}s_i \;=\; \kappa\!\!\sum_{(i,j)\in E(F,O)}\!\!(1-s_i) \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \bar s_F \;\le\; \frac{\kappa}{\mu}\cdot\frac{|E(F,O)|}{|F|}.$$
*(Sum rule verified to $7\times10^{-15}$ across 8 topologies; bound tight within 1.5–2.2x.)* **Containment depends only on cut size and free-set size — nothing about internal module structure.** This is the upper-bound half of what former §5 item 4 requested; paired with the Alon–Boppana floor for random $d$-regular modules it makes Theorem 6 a proven statement rather than an empirical one.

**The vulnerability is real but mis-mechanized in the original flag.** Attack-budget ratio is ~16x (to isolate a module) and ~23x (for equivalent coordination damage). *But* the mechanism the flag anticipated — consensus/relaxation collapse, imported from the cascading-failure literature — is **provably capped**: from Theorem 2, relaxation slowdown is bounded by $1+\kappa\lambda_2^{\text{whole}}/\mu$, which at the design point is $1.116$. **λ₂ collapses 100% while relaxation time degrades at most 11.6%**, because $\mu>0$ floors every mode at rate $\mu$. The imported literature's failure mode does not transfer. The real damage is a **steady-state cross-module bias**, 86–93% of it a rigid module-mean offset, exactly proportional to the target's disagreement across cut bridges — so an orphaned module retains local competence and loses *registration* with the global objective.

**The attack is partially self-defeating** (as Section 3.6 speculated, now confirmed): cutting bridges *improves* containment (escape $0.0655\to0.0484$ isolating one module, $\to 0$ cutting all), forced by Theorem 8 since the attack only reduces $|E(F,O)|$. It cannot propagate corruption — only orphan a module. The dangerous composition is therefore *cut, then corrupt the now-unreachable module*, which by Theorem 7 is precisely the state with zero external correction authority.

**A methodological warning that invalidates a natural experiment design.** Damage scales with $(T_i-T_j)$ across cut bridges, so the budget ratio is ~23x for a **module-structured** target but only ~1.9x for an **i.i.d.** target. Every existing script in `experiments/` uses i.i.d. targets, which would measure ~1.9x and **wrongly conclude the vulnerability is negligible**. Any evaluation must test both.

**Two cheap fixes, both recommended.** (1) Sample bridge endpoints as a *matching* rather than independently — under the current sampler $1-(1-1/m)^M = 0.656$ of instantiations (measured 0.630 over 300 seeds) contain a module severable by a **single** node-gate flip, and the published seed-9 instance is a lucky draw that understates this. (2) Scale $b$ with $m$ at fixed $b/m$: Theorem 8's bound is homogeneous of degree zero in $(b,m)$, so co-scaling holds containment *exactly* constant while raising the min-cut linearly — verified flat at escape $0.066\pm0.002$ with min-cut $2\to8$. **The bridge vulnerability is a small-$N$ artifact that dissolves with scale, provided $b$ is not held fixed at 1.**

**Revised threat model.** For a trained network the coupling graph is architectural, so an arbitrary edge-cutter is unrealistic. The realistic surface is the runtime gate $g_i$: a compromised or merely over-eager quarantine mechanism driving $g_i\to0$ at bridge endpoints obtains the full bridge attack for 1–2 node compromises. Relatedly, a Type-B defector (`v1.md` §3.2) *sitting at a bridge endpoint* has ~5x the corruption gain of an average node ($s\approx0.35$ vs $0.07$) — conjectured from the sum rule, untested, and the sharpest remaining open item.

## 4. Related work — honest positioning

The graph-Laplacian-regularized completion machinery (Theorem 3, the linear-system form) is not new: it is the same equation family as Zhou, Bousquet, Lal, Weston & Schölkopf's "Learning with Local and Global Consistency" (NeurIPS 2004) and Smola & Kondor's "Kernels and Regularization on Graphs" (2003), both foundational to graph-based semi-supervised learning. The empirical connectivity-governs-degradation finding (Section 3.1) is a specific instance of oversmoothing/oversquashing, studied extensively in graph neural network theory via Dirichlet-energy bounds tied to algebraic connectivity (Oono & Suzuki, 2020) and via curvature/bottleneck arguments (Topping et al., "Understanding Oversquashing and Bottlenecks on Graphs via Curvature," 2021). The exact mechanism identified in Theorem 5 — a killed random walk's escape probability, resolvent of $(\mu I+\kappa L_{FF})$ — is classical potential theory / electrical network theory (grounded Laplacians, hitting probabilities, effective resistance), not a new mathematical object. The compartmentalized-topology resolution (Section 3.6) is likewise built on established foundations, not invented whole-cloth: Simon's "The Architecture of Complexity" (1962) introduced near-decomposability — systems whose components interact densely within subsystems and sparsely across them — as a near-universal feature of complex systems, precisely because it lets subsystems adapt or recover independently without destabilizing the whole; modern network science has extended this into explicit resilience/cascading-failure results for modular networks (e.g., work on the resilience of modular complex networks and on hierarchical modularity in brain functional networks), including the caveat we also flag, that inter-module bridge edges can become a concentrated point of failure for global coordination even as they protect against within-module cascade.

What we add: (i) the closed form for this specific composed energy, including the gated $g_i$ quarantine variable (not present in the standard graph-regularization, GNN-oversmoothing, or potential-theory literature, and specifically motivated by the defection/verification framing developed in `v1.md`–`v3.md`); (ii) confirmation that the local priors driving this system can be reached by a provably-local, EqProp-based learning rule rather than a hand-designed kernel or globally-backpropagated loss; (iii) the connection to this investigation's Claim A / Claim B distinction (`discussion1.md` §7) — this entire apparatus is a Claim-A tool (converging to and robustly maintaining an externally-supplied target), not a route to originating one; (iv) the explicit identification, via Theorem 5 and the topology experiment in Section 3.3, of a tension between the connectivity properties that help `v1.md`'s ablation-recovery story and those that help denoising robustness, immediately followed by (v) its resolution via a concrete, measured, compartmentalized design (Section 3.6) — the combination of precisely locating a real architectural trade-off and then closing it with a tested design change, rather than leaving it as a reported limitation, is the paper's central methodological contribution.

## 5. What's still needed for a fully rigorous submission

1. **The 7.5x headline is conditional on corruption respecting module boundaries** (Section 3.6b, threat model T3): under a scattering adversary the modular graph converges to the non-modular value and modularity provides almost no protection. This is the single most important caveat on the design recommendation, and it mirrors the clustered-vs-scattered boundary in Section 3.5.
2. **The small residual $N$-dependence for the grid at fixed coverage fraction (Section 3.4) is not fully explained.** Escape probability rose slightly (not the flat result the corrected local-density theory would most simply predict) as $N$ grew from 36 to 400. The local-density explanation accounts for why it doesn't *fall* (ruling out the original, wrong "bigger domain buffers better" intuition), but a precise account of the small residual rise — plausibly a boundary-node-count effect (more distinct scattered O-nodes reachable, not just a fixed nearest one, as $N$ grows at fixed density) — is stated as a hypothesis here, not proven.
2. **A genuine multi-pattern extension** — the current construction (Theorem 1's strict convexity) provably supports only a single learned target per unit; extending to multiple recallable patterns requires relaxing to non-convex, multi-well local energies, reopening the basin-of-attraction analysis flagged in the earlier discussion, and is left as the natural next step rather than addressed here.
3. **A task beyond synthetic Gaussian patterns** — testing completion/denoising on a real structured dataset would be necessary to claim practical, not just mathematical, relevance.
4. ~~A formal treatment of the ablation-robustness/denoising-robustness tension~~ — **resolved.** Empirically by Section 3.6, then *proven* by Theorem 8's containment sum rule (upper-bounding escape by cut size / free-set size) paired with the Alon–Boppana floor on within-module $\lambda_2$. Theorem 6 is no longer informal.
5. ~~The bridge-vulnerability caveat is untested~~ — **tested and resolved in Section 3.7**, with the anticipated mechanism (consensus collapse) *falsified* by the paper's own Theorem 2 (capped at 11.6%) and replaced by the correct one (steady-state cross-module bias). What remains open: the Type-B-defector-at-a-bridge-endpoint conjecture (~5x corruption gain), which is stated but untested.
6. ~~Module size and count were not optimized or swept~~ — **resolved in Section 3.6b.** A closed-form law ($\bar s \approx 2fb\kappa/(m\mu(1+\kappa r_c))$, validated to $\pm2\%$) shows the advantage ratio is governed by $m/b$, not $N$; all three scaling regimes are derived and independently verified. Optimal module size is threat-model-dependent: $m^\star=c$ under an exogenous correlated budget, minimal under the endogenous reading (where the conjectured trade-off does not exist), and nearly irrelevant under an unconstrained adversary.
7. **The degree-matching and per-module-$\lambda_2$ corrections in Section 3.6 mean the headline 8x is not a clean controlled measurement.** A degree-matched re-run at multiple $N$ is required before any specific factor is quoted quantitatively.
